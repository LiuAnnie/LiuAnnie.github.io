Original Plan

My original plan was to make an interactive website that would control the speed of the 3 servos and lighting. I wanted to replace the flashlight that I use as a light source, with LED strips. This way, I would be able to program various lighting effects and control light from all 4 sides of the instrument. Shown above was my initial sketch for the website: a slider for speed and various buttons to control lighting, including effects and light from each side. However, upon further experimentation, I recognized that the results achieved from the LED strips could not compare to those of the flashlight.

Future Redesign: Lighting + Flashlight Machine

After experimentation, I realized that I didn't want to continue down the LED strip path so I scrapped that part of the original idea from my final project. I ideated other ways I might want to control lighting from my website in the future. I thought of a machine that I could attach the flashlight to and place on top of the instrument. This machine would receive a signal from the web and move the flashlight to the desired spot over the instrument's frame, using x and y position inputs.

Technical Write Up

Moving forward, the concept of my final project is to make an interactive website that will allow me to control my light painting instrument from my computer. I wanted to be able to control the speed of the 3 servos with a slider.

I first reprogrammed the instrument to meet the needs of this project. After making sure that the instrument functioned as intended, I began building the interactive website. I explored using buttons to change the speed of the servos, before I eventually went with a slider.

On the P5 side of the code, I created a slider that can have values 1 to 5 with 3 being the default value. The value of the slider corresponds to the servos' speed settings, with 1 being the slowest and 5 being the fastest. When the value of the slider changes, I send the updated speed setting value through serial to the Arduino.

On the Arduino side of the code, the servos are told to continuously loop back and forth between positions 0 and 180. At the same time, the Arduino is constantly checking for new serial data. When it receives a new speed setting value, each servo's assigned delays are reassigned to give it a new speed. When a '1' comes through over serial, each servo's delay is set to 40 ms, and when a '5' comes through, each servo's delay is set to 5 ms. The longer the delay, the slower the servo will move to its next position.

Pretty straightforward!
